Ringallets — Force Analysis

A walkthrough of the three free-body diagrams, the equilibrium equations that fall out of them, and the safe working load they imply.

Sand-cast aluminum FIG-standard ring 1×1 in pegs · friction fit Static + dynamic loading

1. The System

Front view of the assembly: two vertical legs (milled aluminum), a sand-cast aluminum ring between them, and two integrated 1 in × 1 in pegs at the ring's left and right midline (3 and 9 o'clock). Each peg is hand-pressed into its leg with about 1 in of insertion depth — no glue, pin, or bolt. The ring hangs with clearance below it so a hand can wrap around the bottom. Load P is a downward point force at the bottom of the ring.

Ring (FIG standard)

Inner Ø: 18 cm / 7.09 in
Grip Ø: 2.8 cm / 1.10 in
Centerline span between pegs: L ≈ 8.19 in

Legs & base

Cross-section: 1 in × 1 in, ~12 in tall
Peg center above floor: h ≈ 10 in
Base footprint: ~3 in either side of center

2. FBD — Ring

Isolating the ring: the only external forces acting on it are the applied load P at the bottom and the reactions from the two pegs at the midline (point A on the left, point B on the right). Each peg can transfer both a vertical (Ay, By) and a horizontal (Ax, Bx) reaction.

Vertical equilibrium

ΣFy = 0 → Ay + By − P = 0

By symmetry of the geometry and loading:

Ay = By = P / 2

Horizontal equilibrium

ΣFx = 0 → Ax = Bx (call this magnitude H)

Because the load sits below the line A–B, the ring acts like a shallow two-pin arch. Treating the lower half as an arch with span L = 2r and rise = r (where r is the ring centerline radius), with a point load at midspan, the horizontal thrust is:

H = M₀ / rise = (P · L / 4) / r = (P · 2r / 4) / r = P / 2

So:

Ax = Bx = P / 2

Resultant per-peg force

F_peg = √(Ax² + Ay²) = √((P/2)² + (P/2)²) ≈ 0.707 P

This combined load — and its horizontal component in particular — is what stresses the friction fit and bends the legs.

3. FBD — Legs

By Newton's third law, the ring pushes back on the legs at the peg interface with the same magnitudes but opposite directions. At the base of each leg, the floor provides a normal force N upward and a friction force F directed inward (resisting the leg's tendency to spread outward).

Equilibrium of the left leg

ΣFy = 0 → N₁ − Ay = 0 → N₁ = P / 2 ΣFx = 0 → F₁ − Ax = 0 → F₁ = P / 2

The right leg mirrors this, so N₂ = P/2 and F₂ = P/2. Each foot needs to carry half the weight vertically and resist half the load horizontally.

Required friction coefficient (no-slip condition)

μ_required ≥ F₁ / N₁ = (P/2) / (P/2) = 1.0

That is high — bare aluminum on most floors won't reach it. Rubber feet on a grippy mat can. Without a crossbar tying the two legs together, sliding can become the limit before any material fails.

Bending moment at the base of the leg

The horizontal reaction Ax = P/2 applied at peg height h creates a cantilever moment at the base:

M_leg = Ax · h = (P / 2) · 10 in = 5 P (lb·in)

5. Failure-Mode Calculations

Using the reactions from the two FBDs above, every failure mode reduces to one stress check.

5.1 Ring bending

Treat the lower ring path as a simply supported beam of span L = 8.19 in with a center point load P. Solid round cross-section, grip diameter d = 1.10 in:

M_max = P · L / 4 = 2.05 P I = π d⁴ / 64 = 0.0725 in⁴ c = d/2 = 0.551 in σ_ring = M·c / I = (2.05 P)(0.551) / 0.0725 ≈ 15.6 P psi

5.2 Peg shear

Each peg carries Ay = P/2 in vertical shear over a 1 in² cross-section:

τ_peg = (P / 2) / 1 in² = 0.5 P psi

Even at P = 500 lb that is 250 psi — negligible compared with aluminum shear strength. Not the limiting mode.

5.3 Leg bending ← governs

From the leg FBD: M_leg = 5 P lb·in. Section modulus of a 1×1 square:

S = b·h² / 6 = 1·(1)² / 6 = 0.167 in³ σ_leg = M_leg / S = 5 P / 0.167 = 30 P psi

Because the legs are slender (1×1) and the moment arm is long (10 in), they are 30× more stress-loaded per pound of P than the ring body — a factor of 30 / 15.6 ≈ 1.9 in stress, but the section modulus difference makes leg bending fail first.

5.4 Friction-fit pullout (un-tested)

The peg is hand-pressed only. The horizontal reaction Ax = P/2 tries to pull the peg out of the leg sideways. Without a measured pullout force, this is a known unknown — flagged as a candidate failure mode pending a test.

5.5 Sliding / spreading

From the leg FBD, the floor must supply F₁ = F₂ = P/2 of friction with only N₁ = N₂ = P/2 of normal force, requiring μ ≈ 1.0. Without a crossbar, the legs spread before anything breaks.

Bottom line. The ring body and pegs are not the first failure points. The real constraints are leg bending, friction-fit retention, and outward spreading — all of which are visible directly in the leg FBD as Ax, Bx, F₁, and F₂.

7. How These Numbers Were Reached

  1. Defined the load case. Point load P at the bottom of the ring; pegs are the supports.
  2. Drew the ring FBD. Solved ΣFy and ΣFx → Ay = By = P/2 and Ax = Bx = P/2 from arch action.
  3. Drew the leg FBDs. Newton's third law transferred those reactions onto the legs; the floor provided N and F.
  4. Translated each reaction into a stress check. Ring bending from M = PL/4, peg shear from Ay/A, leg bending from Ax·h/S, friction from F/N.
  5. Applied material brackets for sand-cast aluminum (13 / 20 / 24 ksi) with SF = 3.
  6. Identified leg bending as the governing mode — safe working load ≈ 125–150 lb per Ringallet.
  7. Cross-checked against the prototype — 160 lb held statically, consistent with the analytical range.

8. Highest-Impact Design Improvements

  1. Pin or bolt the peg through the leg — eliminates the friction-fit unknown.
  2. Increase leg cross-section to 1.5 × 1.5 in — section modulus scales with size cubed, so leg bending capacity multiplies by ~3.4.
  3. Add a bottom crossbar tying the two legs together — directly absorbs Ax and Bx instead of relying on floor friction.
  4. Add rubber feet / wider base — raises effective μ and resists tipping.
  5. Add a shoulder/stop on the peg — prevents creep/loosening over time.
