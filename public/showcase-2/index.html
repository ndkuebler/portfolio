WaterShield — Force Analysis

Cantilever beam model PLA, vertical print V1 vs. V2

A one-page engineering breakdown of the forces on the WaterShield longboard fender, comparing the original one-piece PLA body (V1) against the two-body friction-fit + dual D-shaft design (V2).

1. The Setup

The WaterShield mounts to the underside of a longboard deck with two vertically stacked screws and cantilevers off the edge of the board. The curved arc hangs in free space behind the wheel.

Mechanically, both versions behave as a cantilever beam:

  • Fixed support = the two screws / board contact area
  • Free end = tip of the arc
  • Load = vertical impulses from road bumps, vibration, water, or accidental ground contact

Bending moment is highest near the mount, not at the tip.

Why V2 survives

The external tip force is essentially the same in both versions — same rider, same board, same bump produces the same input. What changed is how the moment is reacted:

  • V1 forces the entire bending moment through one continuous, vertically-printed PLA section. Layer lines run perpendicular to the bending stress — the worst orientation for PLA. A small tip force becomes a large bending stress at one fragile cross-section.
  • V2 converts the bending moment into a force couple across two D-shafts spaced 1" apart, plus the friction-fit contact area between the two bodies. The PLA is loaded in bearing — a much friendlier mode for printed plastic — instead of cross-layer tension.

Estimated real ride loads

Hard riding bumps probably apply ~5–10 lbf at the tip, occasionally higher on a sharp hit. That sits above V1's fatigue threshold but well below V2's joint capacity — exactly matching the field results.

Conservative effective stresses used for vertical-print PLA. Tightening the estimate would require: exact crack-section thickness, infill %, wall count, and a measured distance from crack location to arc tip.
