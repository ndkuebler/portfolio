2. Selectivity — why the cable doesn't clump straight

Every magnet is a 3 × 1 mm N42 disc, embedded with its pole axis perpendicular to the cable's local tangent and perpendicular to the natural curl plane of the shape-set helix. Call that direction the local binormal b̂. All magnets share the same +b̂ orientation when the cable is laid flat.

Straight cable

Adjacent magnets sit at separation P along the cable's tangent direction t̂, with poles oriented along b̂ ⟂ t̂. This is the side-by-side, like-parallel dipole configuration. The force between two parallel dipoles with moments m separated by r perpendicular to their axes is:

F∥(r) = -3μ0m² / (4πr⁴) [repulsive when poles aligned]

For our parameters (m ≈ 8.4 × 10⁻³ A·m², r = P = 59 mm), this evaluates to F∥ ≈ 5 × 10⁻⁶ N — five micronewtons of repulsion. Negligible mechanically, but the sign matters: there is no attractive force pulling adjacent magnets together along the straight cable. The cable doesn't see itself.

Coiled cable

Turn n+1 sits axially above turn n by one cable diameter (~3 mm) plus the inter-turn jacket gap. A magnet at angular position θ on turn n with its +b̂ axis pointing along the coil axis sits axially adjacent to its counterpart on turn n+1, whose +b̂ also points along the coil axis — meaning the south face of turn n+1's magnet faces the north face of turn n's magnet. This is the coaxial, head-to-tail configuration:

Fcoax(g) = 6μ0m² / (4πg⁴) [attractive]

At g = 1.0 mm air gap, this evaluates to Fcoax ≈ 0.25 N per pair. Six orders of magnitude stronger than the straight-cable interaction, and in the opposite sign.

The selectivity result. Identical magnets, identical orientation. The straight cable sees a 5 µN repulsion that vanishes with any motion. The coiled cable sees a 0.25 N attraction holding each turn pair together. Selectivity comes from the 1/r⁴ geometry of dipole forces interacting with the helix's spacing, not from coded magnetization. The cable can be built from a single inexpensive SKU.

What about non-adjacent magnets?

Magnets on the same turn (4 per turn at 90° apart on a 75 mm coil) are ~58 mm apart. Magnets two turns away are at ~6 mm axial separation but in a quadrupolar arrangement that nets to ~0.005 N — present, but small enough that the dominant interaction in the coiled state is the nearest-neighbor turn-pair. The coil stack behaves like a chain, not a block.

3. Force budget

Why peel, not bulk break

When the cable extends, pairs do not all release at once. The geometry of pulling the connector outward forces one pair to fail before the next pair sees any load — the lever arm changes the angle of attack on each successive pair sequentially. This is the same principle behind why a strip of velcro peels easily but resists shear pull.

Fextend(t) ≈ Fpair for as long as turns remain coiled

Cable-weight sanity check

A 2 m USB-C cable + magnets ≈ 65 g, weight ≈ 0.64 N. Coil hold of 4.0 N gives a static safety factor of ~6 against the cable hanging vertically from a single pair. The coil cannot fall apart from gravity alone, even held by one bond.

4. EMI and external interference

MacBook lid Hall sensor

The MacBook detects lid-closed via a Hall sensor near the hinge that responds to a magnet in the opposing case. Helix's small N42 discs produce a measured field that drops off rapidly with distance. The free-space dipole field magnitude along the pole axis is:

|B(r)| = (μ0 / 4π) · (2m / r³)

For a 3 × 1 mm N42 disc with m ≈ 8.4 × 10⁻³ A·m²:

Distance from magnetAxial fieldComment
1 mm (paired across air gap)≈ 1700 mTOperating field — design point
10 mm≈ 1.7 mTSkin surface near the coil — well below biosafety thresholds
50 mm≈ 13 µTComparable to Earth's field (~50 µT)
100 mm≈ 1.7 µTNegligible. MacBook Hall sensor at typical bag-carry distance.

The MacBook Hall sensor trigger threshold is ≈ 1 mT. The coiled Helix would have to be pressed directly against the lid hinge to risk a false trigger. In normal bag carry, distances are 50+ mm and the field is three orders of magnitude below threshold.

Magnetic media adjacent to coil

  • Credit card mag stripe: demagnetization threshold ≈ 30 mT. Helix's surface field at 10 mm is 1.7 mT — well below. A card in the same pocket as a Helix coil is safe.
  • Hard disk drives: sealed shielding plus internal field gradients. External fields under 5 mT do not affect data integrity. Helix is below this at all reasonable separations.
  • Hotel room keys (mag-stripe variants): same threshold class as credit cards. Safe.
  • Pacemakers / ICDs: regulatory guidance (FDA, 2021) recommends 6 inches (152 mm) of separation from devices with magnets ≥10 G surface field. Helix's per-magnet surface field is ~170 G but drops to <1 G at 100 mm. A label callout matching MagSafe's pacemaker warning is appropriate.

USB-C signal integrity

USB-C data lines are differential pairs (D+/D−, TX/RX, SBU). A static DC magnetic field induces no EMF on the conductors. The only concern is mechanical: the magnet inclusions must not deform the conductor geometry beyond impedance spec (90 Ω ± 5 Ω differential for USB 2.0; 80 Ω ± 4 Ω for SuperSpeed). The magnet pocket layer sits outside the braid shield, so conductor geometry is undisturbed.

5. Design for Manufacturing

This is where the design earns or loses its right to exist. The bill of materials is cheap; the assembly is not trivial.

Process flow

  1. Cable core: standard USB-C 240W conductor stack with aramid braid shield — manufactured exactly as today, no change.
  2. Inner jacket (PA12): co-extruded over the braid with pre-formed magnet pockets at pitch P = 59 mm. Pocket geometry is a 3.2 × 1.2 mm cylindrical recess oriented to the cable's permanent reference flat.
  3. Magnet insertion: automated pick-and-place from a tape-and-reel carrier (SMD-style). The carrier pre-orients every magnet so the same pole faces "up" relative to the cable's reference flat. Insertion uses a non-magnetic ceramic tooling head to avoid stripping magnets off the pick nozzle.
  4. Vision QC pass 1: 100% inline check of magnet presence, position (±0.5 mm), and pole orientation (±5°). Orientation is confirmed by a small Hall sensor array reading the field signature, not by visual inspection.
  5. Outer jacket overmold (TPE): seals the magnet pockets. Standard cable overmold step, no novel tooling.
  6. Helical shape-set: the cable is wound around a 75 mm OD heated mandrel at 110 °C for 90 s, then cooled to lock in the helical resting shape. This is the last step before connector overmolding so the cable is straight (uncoiled) for connector handling.
  7. Connector overmold + final test: standard.

The magnet magazine problem

The hardest unsolved-by-default piece is keeping 34 identical magnets in identical orientation through pick-and-place. Magnets in a bulk hopper will rotate, repel, and chain. Solution: order magnets from the supplier already on tape-and-reel with pre-aligned orientation, the way SMD components ship. This is a real product offered by several neodymium suppliers (K&J, Stanford Magnets) at a 15–25¢/unit premium over loose stock. Worth it.

Tolerance stack

DimensionToleranceFailure mode if violated
Magnet pitch P± 0.5 mmPairs miss across turns — coil "skips" registration
Magnet rotational orientation± 5°Pole alignment drifts; peel force becomes nonuniform
Helix natural diameter D± 3 mmCoil circumference no longer matches n·P — same as pitch error

Magnet inclusion vs. conductor≥ 0.5 mm radial clearanceMechanical: avoid conductor crush; signal: impedance disturbance

The dominant tolerance challenge is matching pitch P to coil circumference C. If C ≠ n·P, every successive turn drifts angularly and the cable cannot register. Both numbers must hold to ±0.5 mm over a 2 m run. The coil circumference is set by the shape-set mandrel diameter and the jacket's thermal hysteresis — both are well-characterized process parameters. The pitch is set by extrusion line speed × pocket-cutting wheel frequency, also tight.

8. Summary and what I'd want to argue about

The interesting result of this analysis is that the magnetics close cleanly with cheap, identical, off-the-shelf neodymium discs. The selectivity that prevents the cable from clumping is geometric, not magnetic. The hard problems are not in the physics — they're in:

  1. Holding the pitch-vs-circumference relationship over a 2 m run.
  2. Avoiding fatigue concentration at the magnet inclusion sites in the conductor stack.
  3. The shape-set jacket's long-term curl retention under thermal cycling.

Those are real PDE problems. They are the same problems a Cable Accessories team has been solving for fifteen years, just applied to a new feature. That's the part I'd want to talk through with someone who has shipped a cable.
