Helix: a self-coiling MacBook charging cable

How I'd approach designing a charging cable that organizes itself: the first idea that broke, the architecture I'd commit to, and where I'd expect to be wrong.

Paper concept, not built. The render below is a visualization. Numbers on this page are calculated from dipole physics and N42 spec sheets, not bench measurements. The point of this writeup is the thinking: how I'd frame the problem, where I'd pivot, and what I'd test next.

Concept Engineering Analysis

The problem

The MacBook charging cable is in millions of bags. It tangles in every one of them. Coiling it manually unravels the moment the bag moves. The fixes that exist (velcro straps, silicone cinches, retractable enclosures) all require a second object you can lose or a deliberate two-handed action. The cable should organize itself.

My first instinct (and where it falls apart)

The obvious first move: embed a row of identical magnets along the cable, evenly spaced, and let them latch wherever they meet.

Thinking it through, this doesn't work. Magnets attract indiscriminately. The cable would find the densest configuration it can: a knot, not a coil.

That reframes the problem on paper. I don't need more magnets. I need selective magnets: pairs that only latch with the right partner, in the right place.

Three ways to get selectivity

  1. Coded multipole magnets. Printed N/S patterns that only mate with a mirror partner (Polymagnets). Cleanest physics. Too expensive and too fragile to manufacture at cable density.
  2. Polarity sequencing. Every magnet is identical and cheap, but their orientation and spacing are tuned so that on a straight cable they mildly repel, and on a coiled cable they attract across each turn-gap. Selectivity comes from geometry, not from coded magnets.
  3. Magnets plus soft-iron slugs. Alternate real magnets with passive steel along the cable. A magnet sticks to a slug, not to another magnet. Simplest, automatic, half the magnet cost, but the snap-feel is softer and coil registration is less crisp.

I picked #2, with #3 as a known fallback. The tradeoff I'd take to a design review: option #2 buys the cleanest snap-feel and the simplest supply chain (one SKU, one supplier, one inspection step), at the cost of a harder magnetic-orientation problem to solve on the factory floor. Option #3 trades a softer feel for a meaningfully cheaper BOM and a simpler line. Same cable architecture either way. That's the decision I'd want to make alongside whoever owns cost, not on my own.
