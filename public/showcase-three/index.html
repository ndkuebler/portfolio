Helix — a self-coiling charging cable

A MacBook charging cable that latches itself into a tidy spiral when you put it down, and unspools one click at a time when you pull. Powered by selectively paired magnets, not springs, motors, or shape memory alone.

Concept Engineering Analysis

1. The problem worth solving

The MacBook charging cable is in millions of bags. It tangles in every one of them. Coiling it manually is annoying, and any human-applied coil unravels the moment the bag moves. The cable's natural enemy isn't damage. It's chaos.

There are existing fixes — velcro straps, silicone cinches, retractable enclosures — and none have caught on. They all require a second object you can lose or a deliberate two-handed action. The cable should organize itself.

Design target. The cable should auto-coil into a pocket-sized spiral within two seconds of being released, hold that coil through a backpack commute, and uncoil one click at a time when pulled. No second object. No motor. No batteries. No instructions.

2. Why magnets are the right answer (and why they're the hard answer)

Magnets are appealing because they hold without contact, store no energy, and never wear out. The catch is that magnets attract indiscriminately. Embed a row of identical magnets along a cable and you don't get a coil — you get a knot. The cable bunches into the densest configuration it can find, regardless of geometry.

The interesting engineering question becomes: can the magnets be made to pair only with the right partner?

Three approaches considered

A. Coded multipole magnets

Each magnet has a printed N/S pattern (a magnetic "key"). Two magnets only latch when their patterns are mirrored partners. Commercial offerings exist (Correlated Magnetics / Polymagnets).

✗ Exotic, expensive, fragile to manufacture at cable density. Not DFM-friendly.

B. Polarity sequencing

Every magnet is identical and inexpensive. Selectivity comes from how they are oriented along the cable's length, combined with cable pitch. Magnets at the right helix separation attract; magnets in the wrong relationship to each other don't.

✓ Cheap parts, geometry does the work. Manufacturing complexity moves to orientation control.

C. Magnets + ferrous slugs

Alternate real magnets with soft-iron slugs along the cable. A magnet sticks to a slug but not to another magnet. Selectivity is automatic.

✓ Simplest. Half the magnetic content. Coil registration is less crisp and the snap-feel is softer.

I chose approach B (polarity sequencing) for the main design, with approach C noted as a viable manufacturing-cost alternative that could win on a BOM cost trade-study. Both deserve to live in the same design review.

3. The Helix architecture

The cable does two things at once: it wants to be coiled, and it locks when coiled.

Wants to be coiled — shape-set TPE jacket

The outer jacket is a thermoplastic elastomer wound around a small heated mandrel during the final cure step. Like the spiral cord of a 1970s telephone handset, the jacket's relaxed energy state is a helix, not a straight line. Pulled straight, it stores elastic energy. Released, it snaps back. This is mature, well-understood manufacturing.

Locks when coiled — paired magnetic latches

Identical N42 neodymium disc magnets (3 mm × 1 mm) are embedded in the jacket at a fixed pitch P along the cable's length, with their pole axes aligned along the local binormal of the natural helix — that is, pointing along what becomes the helix's central axis once the cable coils up.

Here is what that buys:

  • When coiled: the magnet at angular position θ on turn n sits axially next to the magnet at the same θ on turn n+1, north face to south face across a small air gap. They attract. Every turn latches to the turn above and below it.
  • When straight: adjacent magnets along the cable are spaced by P with their pole axes parallel and same-direction. In that side-by-side configuration, like-poles face the same way and the dominant interaction is mild repulsion, not attraction. The cable doesn't clump.

4. Cable cross-section

Standard USB-C 240W (EPR) conductor stack stays intact. The novel content lives in the jacket.

Failure modes the user should never notice

  • A turn skips a register and pairs misalign by one pitch: the next coil-down realigns by itself (geometry is self-correcting).
  • Cable gets tangled around something else in the bag (the headphones): the magnets release individually as the user pulls — no kinks, no permanent set.
  • Adjacent equipment near the coil (credit card, USB drive): see Engineering Analysis §4. Field at 25 mm is below the threshold for any consumer-grade magnetic media.

6. What I would prototype first

A working bench prototype isn't 80 magnets in a finished cable. It's a 30 cm test section with twelve magnets, hand-assembled in a 3D-printed jig, used to validate three things in this order:

  1. Orientation tolerance. How much rotational error in magnet placement can the system absorb before pairs miss each other across turns? This sets the manufacturing inspection budget.
  2. Peel force vs. air gap. Measure single-pair release across a sweep of jacket overmold thicknesses (0.5 / 0.75 / 1.0 / 1.5 mm). Find the sweet spot between "too easy to uncoil" and "fights to extend."
  3. Shape-set retention after thermal cycling. The TPE helix is the actual coil driver. Verify that 1,000 cycles at 50 °C (worst-case hot car) doesn't soften the curl below the magnet hold threshold.

If those three pass, the rest is industrialization, not invention.

7. Open questions and honest limits

Things I would want a real PDE conversation about, not things I'm pretending I've solved:

  • Effect of magnet inclusion on cable bend radius and conductor fatigue life. Each magnet is a local stiffness discontinuity — needs a graded-durometer transition or it becomes a stress riser.
  • Hall-sensor interference with the MacBook lid-closed magnet, if the cable rests against the closed lid. Distance is on Helix's side, but it needs to be measured, not asserted.
  • Cost adder vs. perceived value: a $19 cable replacement with a magnetic spine is plausible; a $39 one is not. The BOM impact of 80 N42 discs at scale needs a real costing pass before this concept earns a charter.
  • Whether approach C (magnets + ferrous slugs) wins on cost without losing enough of the snap-feel to matter. That's a perceptual study, not a calculation.
