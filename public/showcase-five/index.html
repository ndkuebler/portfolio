EarFit

Predict your AirPods Pro tip size from a photo of your ear. Upload a side-profile photo, place a few landmarks, and the tool estimates which silicone tip should fit you best.

Concept prototype by Nick Kuebler. Not affiliated with Apple. Uses your iris as a millimeter scale reference and four hand-placed ear landmarks to estimate your ear canal entrance, then maps the result to a tip size using a published anthropometric ear-canal distribution.

Step 1 of 4

Upload a side-profile photo

Take a 3/4 view photo of your head so that both your ear and at least one eye are clearly visible. A pure side view makes the iris hard to see, which we need for scale.

Step 2 of 4

Drag the two blue dots to the horizontal edges of your iris (the colored circle around your pupil). Adult iris diameter is 11.7 mm and is consistent across people, so this gives us a millimeter scale for the rest of the photo.

How it works

1. Iris calibration

The horizontal diameter of an adult human iris is 11.7 mm, with a standard deviation of about 0.4 mm. This is so consistent across people that ophthalmologists use it as a built-in scale reference in clinical imaging. By marking the iris in your photo, we get the millimeter-per-pixel conversion for everything else in the image.

2. Ear dimensions extracted

From the four hand-placed ear landmarks we compute:

  • Ear length — top of helix to bottom of lobe
  • Canal entrance proxy — distance from tragus to antitragus, a reasonable 2D estimate of the canal opening visible from the side
  • Concha region size — derived from landmark spacing

3. Mapping to a tip size

AirPods Pro ship with four silicone tips (XS, S, M, L) whose outer diameters are roughly 9, 10, 11, and 12 mm. A good acoustic seal needs the tip to be slightly larger than the canal entrance so the silicone compresses against the canal wall. The tool maps the estimated canal-entrance diameter to the smallest tip that exceeds it by ~2–3 mm.

4. The reference distribution

The histogram on the result screen is a normal distribution centered on a published adult ear-canal-entrance mean (~7 mm) with a standard deviation of ~1.2 mm. The marker shows where your estimated canal entrance falls.

Where this is honest about its limits

  • The canal-entrance proxy from a 2D photo is rough. A real ear canal opening is roughly elliptical and partially occluded by the tragus. The tragus-to-antitragus distance is a 2D shadow of the real 3D opening.
  • Tip choice isn't just canal diameter. Seal depends on canal shape, ear-canal cartilage compliance, and how the tip settles after insertion. A real fit-test is acoustic, not geometric.
  • There's no ground truth in v1. The mapping from canal diameter to tip size is rule-based using published anthropometric data plus reasonable assumptions about Apple's tip sizing strategy. It would need to be calibrated against a labeled dataset (real users + their actual fit-test outcomes) to be more than directional.
  • Manual landmarks are slow. A trained landmark detector would make this a one-tap tool. Manual placement is v1 for transparency: every measurement is something the user explicitly placed.

What I'd build next

  • Train a small ear-landmark detector on a labeled dataset so the four points appear automatically.
  • Add live camera capture with a face guide overlay (3/4 view target on screen).
  • Collect a labeled validation set: ~30–50 real ear photos paired with the wearer's actual AirPods tip size from a fit test. Use it to calibrate the rule-based prediction or train a small classifier.
  • Show a confidence interval based on landmark placement uncertainty rather than a single best-fit answer.
  • Extend beyond AirPods Pro to other in-ear products (Beats Studio Buds, sleep earbuds, hearing aid domes).
